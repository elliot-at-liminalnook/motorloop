A field guide to a robot-learning pipeline

How a simulated quadruped goes from bench-measured motors to a gait.

This project turns a single provenance-tracked config into a GPU physics world where thousands of copies of a robot learn to walk — and then to fight. Follow the whole pipeline below, stopping at the decision point that defines each stage. The through-line, learned the hard way: a simulation is only as honest as its cheapest unverified assumption.

Stage 01 — Modeling

Model the body from measured motors

Before any learning happens, a robot has to exist as physics. That body is grown from one source of truth — and its “muscles” are real motors that were characterized on a bench, not invented.

It starts with a motor that has receipts sim/tests/motors.py

Every actuator traces back to a profiled part. The chosen one is a Waveshare ST3215-HS bus servo, and it is modelled honestly — an integrated servo has no meaningful winding resistance or back-EMF constant at its interface, so it is represented as a torque–speed line, not a fake motor. Each number carries a provenance tag: measured, datasheet, decided, or assumed.

ST3215-HS propertyValueSource

No-load speed11.10 rad/sdatasheet (106 rpm)

Decision pointModel a servo as a torque–speed line, not a motor
ChosenPosition servo clipped to stall torque, derated to zero at no-load speed
vs
RejectedInvent R / L / Ke and drive it like a raw BLDC

Why: the honest interface of an integrated servo is stall-torque and no-load-speed. Faking electrical internals would let a policy exploit torque the hardware can never deliver.

One config generates the whole robot robot.toml → gen_robot_mjcf.py

A single provenance-tracked file, robot.toml, defines the torso, four 3-DOF legs, springs, a pneumatic striker, and the ranges over which the body may be randomized. A generator compiles it to MJCF — the XML MuJoCo actually simulates. The whole point is that swapping a part is a config edit, and the total mass is budgeted so torso + legs + twelve 68 g servos lands exactly on the 6 lb (2.72 kg) limit.

Each hinge’s peak torque is derived, never guessed: τ = stall_torque × gear. That single relationship links the simulated muscle to the bench motor — the same discipline the firmware side uses to link RTL to its plant.

Decision pointRandomize the body so part-swaps need no retrain
ChosenTrain across a [domain_randomization] range of masses, lengths, stiffness
vs
AltTrain one policy per exact body

Why: a physical swap that stays inside the trained envelope warm-starts for free. Only a change that alters the action dimension — adding a leg or a DOF — forces a from-scratch retrain.

The catch that MuJoCo hides the gear attribute

Here is the line that becomes the whole story of Stage 02. In MuJoCo a motor’s torque is gear × ctrl, the control signal is clamped to ±1, the default gear is 1, and forcerange only clamps — it never amplifies. So the generator must emit gear equal to the derived torque, or the actuator silently collapses to 1 N·m.

generated MJCF · one hinge motorgen_robot_mjcf.py

       gear="5.884"   <!-- MUST equal τ. default=1 ⇒ the bug -->
       ctrlrange="-1 1"
       forcerange="-5.884 5.884"/>

Two bodies, two fidelities parametric vs CAD mesh

There are deliberately two body pipelines. The parametric quadruped uses a uniform 3:1 gear and simple capsules — fast, uniform, the target for most training. The CAD mesh body is the real mechanism authored in Blender, exported as a .physics.json contract, and carries per-axis transmissions that behave very differently:

Why: the polynomial keeps the constraint Jacobian full-rank at the toggle dead-center, so the whole fleet can run at dt = 0.004 s instead of being forced to half that — and the toggle’s force-amplification survives. The worm’s self-locking is modelled as friction, and the belt as a spring, or steering would train unrealistically crisp.

Stage 02 — The plot twist

The one line of XML that crippled every training run

For months, the robots shuffled, scooted and undulated but never truly walked. Serious attempts stalled. A July-2026 audit — 52 agents crawling the codebase adversarially — found the reason, and it wasn’t the learning algorithm.

The MJCF generator never emitted the gear attribute on the hinge motors. Torque is gear × ctrl; control clamps to ±1; the default gear is 1. The design called for roughly 13 N·m per joint. Every joint actually maxed out at 1 N·m.

What the body had
1 N·m
default gear=1 · about 8% of design torque at every hinge
What it needed just to stand
4.8 N·m
measured hold torque at the rear knees in a settle test

Every training run in the project’s history was performed by a robot physically incapable of standing under its own strength. The standing-still exploit, the shuffling, the failed CPG paddle, the warm-start that wouldn’t transfer — all of it happened on a fighter running at 8% of its intended muscle. The RL stack had spent months being psychoanalyzed for a problem that lived in one line of XML.

The cruelest irony: a body-validation script did check torque margin — but it read the limit from the attribute that documented intent (forcerange) instead of commanding the actuator and measuring the force it actually produced. The spec said 13 N·m; the compiled artifact delivered 1; and for the project’s entire lifetime, no test made them meet.

Decision pointVerify outcomes, never attributes
ChosenCommand ctrl=1, step physics, assert the joint force equals τ
vs
RejectedRead the torque limit off the model’s declared attribute

Why: this bug class can only be caught by measuring what the actuator does. A contract test now re-derives τ and even re-strips the gear to prove the check would fail on the historical model. See Stage 08.

Since the audit, the body has been re-specced onto the servo-true mesh leg and then upgraded to a “v2” actuator (peak-factor 6 / gear 10 ⇒ 16.21 N·m, 32 rad/s), passing 18/18 verification tests including an independent cross-engine check. The 8% figure is the audit-era parametric body — the exact number that made the failure legible.
Stage 03 — Simulation

Simulate thousands of worlds at once

Modern robot RL works because of an almost embarrassingly simple trick: don’t simulate one arena — simulate a thousand, as a single tensor operation on a GPU. The learner sees every slightly-different world at the same time.

Physics runs in MuJoCo-Warp (with plain MuJoCo kept as a CPU oracle). Every world differs a little — spawn positions, joint noise, randomized physical parameters — so a single policy has to generalize instead of memorize. Two clocks matter: the physics integrator ticks at 250 Hz (dt = 0.004 s), while the policy only acts at 50 Hz, with the low-level servo controller running every physics substep in between.

Decision pointMuJoCo-Warp over MJX for contact-heavy fights
ChosenWarp — a compacted contact pool (92k env-steps/s on the fight scene)
vs
AltMJX — a trace-frozen worst-case allocation (7.8k env-steps/s)

Why: the fight scene has ~20 real contacts but MJX must pre-allocate for hundreds. Warp’s compaction gives an 11.8× speedup. Fight throughput is flat from 1k–16k worlds (solver-bound), so training runs at 1–4k worlds where the GPU is best utilized.

Everything downstream is functional GPU array math — no Python-controlled loops, no branching on values the GPU hasn’t computed yet. Every algorithm in the later stages had to be expressed as pure tensor operations to survive compilation. That constraint shapes the entire codebase.
Stage 04 — The environment

Define the task: how the robot senses and is scored

A learning environment is three choices: what the policy controls, what it observes, and what it is paid for. This stack follows the Go2/ANYmal recipe that made quadruped RL reliable — and hardens every reward against being gamed.

Control: pose targets, not torques PD servo @ 250 Hz

The policy does not output torques. It outputs small offsets from a standing pose; a position controller turns those into torque every physics substep, with the torque faded to zero as the joint approaches its no-load speed (a real servo can’t push hard and spin fast). Random exploration on pose offsets produces coherent posture perturbations instead of instant collapse.

Decision pointPosition targets → PD → torque
ChosenPolicy emits pose offsets; a PD servo makes torque at 250 Hz
vs
RejectedPolicy emits raw joint torques directly

Why: this single choice is the best-documented uplift in legged RL. It turns noise into wiggling posture rather than falling over, so exploration actually explores gaits.

Observation: an asymmetric actor–critic 50 obs / +34 privileged

The actor — the network that could someday run on real hardware — sees only what a real robot could: 50 dimensions of joint angles, velocities, orientation, its last action, and the velocity command, all rotated into the body frame. In combat it also gets a deliberately-degraded 144-ray lidar (per-ray noise, dropouts read as max-range, a latency buffer). The critic — which only exists during training and never ships — gets 34 extra dimensions of privileged truth: exact contact states, foot penetration, true root velocity.

The coach sees everything; the player sees only the field. The player’s habits must work under fog, but the critic’s feedback is never confused by it.

Reward: many small terms, all adversarially chosen walk mode

The term that actually conjures a gait is air-time: each foot is paid for swing phases of roughly the right duration — and the credit saturates, so hopping can’t out-earn a clean cadence. You cannot farm it without lifting feet in rhythm, and lifting feet in rhythm is walking.

Why: a penalty can be out-earned; a termination cuts the value bootstrap and makes the behavior structurally unprofitable. Slip, tipping, over-speed joints, and standing-still-when-commanded all trigger a stochastic end — the machinery that stops the reward hacks in Stage 07.

The actuator curriculum: a parameter α blends from an ideal actuator (no speed droop) at the start of training to the servo-true torque–speed envelope by 60% through. Learn the gait on an easy motor, then force it to obey the real hardware.
Stage 05 — Learning to walk

Teach a gait, then let PPO refine it

Rather than hope raw reinforcement learning discovers walking from scratch, the stack seeds it with a rhythmic prior, clones a rough policy, then improves it with PPO — the workhorse of robot learning.

A central pattern generator as the teacher cpg_teacher.py

Biological spinal cords hold coupled oscillators that produce rhythmic gaits without the brain micromanaging each muscle. The same idea here is a 35-parameter CPG: base frequency, per-leg phase offsets, duty factor, foot clearance and per-joint sinusoid coefficients, tuned by rollout-grounded search (not by hand). The learned policy outputs a residual on top of this prior, so it starts from a rhythm rather than from noise.

PPO with the four fixes that mattered train_mesh_warp.py

The learner is PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization): let the policy play, nudge it toward better-than-expected actions, but clip the nudge so no single batch can overwrite what it already knows. The audit found the historical runs weren’t just under-torqued — they were under-everything. All four were cheap to fix:

FixBeforeAfter
Torque (the gear)1 N·m~13 N·m
Optimization budget~37 iters100M+ steps
Discount horizon (γ)0.97 · 0.66 s0.99 · ~2.0 s
Network size4×32512·256·128

The old discount factor gave a credit horizon shorter than a single stride — the policy literally could not see far enough ahead to value a footfall. The old network was a 15:1 bottleneck on a 470-dim observation. The current learner uses a squashed-Gaussian policy, GAE (γ=0.99, λ=0.95, clip 0.2), a SiLU (512·256·128) actor and privileged critic, and schedules that anneal exploration, the actuator curriculum, and imitation weight over the run.

Decision pointTeacher → student adaptation (RMA-style)
ChosenPrivileged teacher; a student infers the hidden world from its recent history
vs
AltShip the privileged policy and hope the extra inputs exist on hardware

Why: the deployable student never sees privileged truth, so it learns to estimate physical parameters online from what it can measure. Success is defined as the student closing most of the return gap to the teacher on held-out worlds.

The result: with all four fixes and a green preflight, a 40.5M-step run produced a policy that walks at 0.83 m/s on the deployed route eval, survived a full 600-step episode, and recovered from a stumble without falling — confirmed by rendered video, at 2.8× the 0.3 m/s milestone bar. “The body walks; the gear bug was the whole story.”
Stage 06 — Learning to fight

Learn against an opponent that’s also learning

Walking is a command; fighting is a curriculum. Once a robot can move on demand, combat training layers self-play, a widening opponent pool, and an automated coach on top of the same learner.

Self-play against a hall of fame PFSP league

The policy trains against frozen snapshots of its past selves. Good snapshots are periodically promoted into the opponent pool, and opponents are sampled by Prioritized Fictitious Self-Play: an opponent’s weight rises the harder it is to beat (w ∝ (1−winrate)²). Nothing ever leaves the pool.

Decision pointSample the whole hall of fame, weighted by difficulty
ChosenPFSP over the full archive — hard opponents most often
vs
RejectedBest-respond to the most recent opponent only

Why: chasing one opponent at a time causes cycling — the classic Red-Queen forgetting where a policy peaks then collapses. Keeping the whole archive in support is what stops it.

Walk-then-fight, and exploration as a search dimension

A curriculum first pays the robot for locomotion toward the opponent, then fades combat reward in while widening the spawn distance — and keeps a fraction of episodes as pure walking drills forever, so the fighter can never forget its legs. Three exploration add-ons ride along, and crucially they aren’t hand-tuned — they’re population-training hyperparameters the run discovers per-agent:

RND

Curiosity

Pay a bonus for states a predictor network finds surprising — measured over tactical descriptors (engagement geometry, contact events), not raw joint angles.

HER

Hindsight

If the robot ended somewhere useless, relabel the episode as if that was the goal — learn from the success that accidentally happened.

PBT

Population training

Many runs in parallel; the weak periodically copy the strong and mutate hyperparameters. Tuning happens during training, not between runs.

Coach

Adaptive weights

A control loop reads a held-out benchmark and raises the reward weight on lagging skills — gated by whether that skill is actually learnable right now.

Decision pointLet a coach move reward weights, don’t hand-tune them
ChosenFeedback loop: weight rises on a lagging-but-tractable competency, decays on a satisfied one
vs
AltFix reward weights by hand and re-run on every plateau

Why: a satisfied objective can be over-optimized into a degenerate one; a lagging objective may be unlearnable right now. Reading a held-out benchmark decomposition lets the coach avoid pouring reward into a hole the body can’t climb out of.

Stage 07 — The training ladder

Thirty tasks, easiest to hardest

You do not point reinforcement learning at “win a fight” and wait. The same body climbs a ladder of about thirty tasks whose difficulty rises one axis at a time — hold, then step, then travel, then survive a shove, then chase an opponent that fights back. Each rung warm-starts from the policy below it and carries its own cheap pass-check, so training and verification stay gradual instead of all-or-nothing.

Stage 08 — Verification

Keep it honest: metrics select, videos confirm

RL agents are amoral optimizers of exactly what you wrote. This project was burned twice — metrics claimed “walking,” video falsified it — and responded by treating the simulator itself as something to be audited.

The villain arc: how the agents cheated

Every exploit forced the task definition to get smarter. The catalog is instructive precisely because each one looked great on a scoreboard:

Exploit 01

The catapult

Launched its own torso 1.5 m into the air at the bell, landed all its damage in the spawn chaos, then idled. Beautiful damage ratio; terrible video.

Patched bya torque-ratio band that flags catapult-strength actuation
Exploit 02

The pacifist statue

“Won” by standing perfectly still and letting incidental contact do the scoring.

Patched bya stationary penalty + a min-progress termination
Exploit 03

The oscillator

Farmed a movement-speed reward by vibrating in place — max instantaneous speed, zero displacement.

Patched byrewarding net displacement, penalizing wasted energy
Exploit 04

The curiosity junkie

RND paid novelty for unseen joint angles, so the policy jittered to manufacture novelty without doing anything.

Patched bymeasuring novelty over tactical descriptors, not proprioception

Checkpoint promotion is now gated on trajectory-level behavior — net displacement, genuine approach, path quality — not just score, backed by roughly two dozen “did you actually fight or did you find a loophole” metrics. As the note in the repo puts it: a reward function is a contract written against a very hostile lawyer.

The layered check stack cheapest first

Every layer kills a specific class of bug, ordered so the fastest checks fail first:

T0
Model-build contract
Command ctrl=1, assert the joint delivers τ; assert gear on every hinge, mass, ranges, armature. Kills the gear-bug class on day one.
T1
Static capability
Measure torque by outcome; hold the canonical stance (no adaptive bar); a --strip-gear run must fail. Kills spring-propped false stands.
T2
Training-config preflight
Fail if PPO iterations < 200, if the credit horizon is shorter than a stride, or if a config flag is silently ignored. Kills the 37-iteration, γ=0.97, tiny-net runs.
T3
Mechanism liveness
Resets must differ; every control/obs mode gets a smoke rollout. Kills dead code that exists but never fires.
T4
Reward red-teaming
Pre-registered exploit fixtures (stand-still, oscillate, catapult) must each score less than an honest attempt before any reward change merges. Kills reward hacks pre-GPU.
T7
Checkpoint semantics
Every checkpoint carries a model hash + action/obs semantics; a frozen opponent is rejected on semantics, not just shape. Kills silently mis-driven policies.
Decision pointBreak the “a simulator can’t disagree with itself” trap
ChosenAn independent Drake model re-derives τ from motor primitives and cross-checks
vs
RejectedTrust the compiled model because its own checks pass

Why: shared-fate is exactly how the gear bug survived — the model and its tests read the same wrong number. A second engine that never reads the MJCF agrees to 0.003% on torque, and fires loudly (12.97×) on the stripped model.

The launch gate. One command — run_pre_gpu_tests.sh --require-gpu — is the only complete verdict: dependency contract, plain-MuJoCo vs Warp parity, contract tests, training-scale GPU pytest, and a same-seed determinism canary. A pile of individually-green commands is explicitly not permission to launch.
Stage 08 — Where it stands

Done, in progress, and the frontier

The pipeline is real end to end, and it produced its first honest behavioral milestone in July 2026. The map below is deliberately unglamorous — the project’s rule is that only video counts.

Done

  • Full verification stack: contract tests, preflight, exploit fixtures, goldens, cross-engine check
  • Walks on command at 0.83 m/s, confirmed on rendered video
  • Warp performance ladder complete, with upstream fixes contributed back
  • Body upgraded to v2 — 16.21 N·m, 32 rad/s, 18/18 tests

In progress

  • First two-sided fight: a walking pursuer opponent vs the stronger body
  • A real PFSP league driven by the adaptive coach
  • Whack-a-mole reward hardening: the anti-creep terms revealed a stand-still exploit; a min-progress termination is the next fix

Frontier

  • Open-ended self-play combat — built and tested, not yet a result
  • The real CAD leg: the as-built worm/blade mechanism is a stomp weapon, not yet a walker; a co-design arc points at a BLDC actuator direction
  • Sim-to-real: the current walker is a surrogate; hardware fit is gated on bench measurements
The honest caveat. The 0.83 m/s walker runs on a parametric surrogate body. The actual mechanism in CAD — worm-driven pitch, series-elastic yaw, a toggle-press blade foot — was found to be built for stomping, not striding; its knee does retract enough to lift a foot, and a Drake-certified crawl geometry got feet lifting in sim, but transferring to real hardware remains deliberately gated behind bench measurements. Simulation is an executable claim, not truth.

The through-line of the whole project, if it has one: simulation is only as honest as its cheapest unverified assumption. The firmware side learned that years ago and tests everything. The RL side learned it the hard way — and wrote it down.
